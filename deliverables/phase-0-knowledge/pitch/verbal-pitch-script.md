# Vibhor's Verbal Pitch — Mom Ka Nuska

> **Format:** 5-minute pitch + 10-minute Q&A  
> **Tone:** Confident, warm, data-backed, personal. Not salesy — storytelling with numbers.  
> **Practice:** Read aloud 10+ times. Time yourself. Cut anything over 5 minutes.

---

## THE PITCH (5 Minutes)

### Opening — The Hook (30 seconds)

*[Stand center. Make eye contact. Pause before speaking.]*

> "Namaste. My name is Vibhor Singh.
>
> I want you to close your eyes for a second and think about the last time you ate something that tasted exactly like your mom made it.
>
> Not 'close enough.' Not 'similar flavor.' EXACTLY like her kitchen.
>
> You probably can't remember — because it doesn't exist in a packet.
>
> That's the problem we're solving."

---

### The Problem (45 seconds)

> "India's packaged food industry is ₹5.5 lakh crore. And it's built on one big lie.
>
> Every brand says 'ghar jaisa,' 'maa ke haath ka,' 'homestyle.' But open the back of the packet — preservatives, stabilizers, flavor enhancers. A factory in Noida is not your mother's kitchen.
>
> Here's what shocked me: 67% of Indian consumers — that's a national FSSAI survey — say they don't trust ingredient labels. Two-thirds of the country thinks their food brands are lying to them.
>
> And they're right.
>
> There's a ₹5.5 lakh crore industry with a massive trust deficit. Nobody has fixed it yet."

---

### The Solution (60 seconds)

> "So we asked a simple question: What if, instead of pretending to be homemade — we actually were?
>
> Meet my mother — Anjula Singh.
>
> *[Point to slide / hold up photo]*
>
> She has been cooking for over 30 years. Her achar, her papad, her masalas — these are recipes that three generations of our family have fought over at the dinner table.
>
> We didn't hire a food scientist. We didn't license a recipe. We gave my mother her own farm, her own kitchen, and said: 'Make what you've always made. We'll handle the rest.'
>
> The brand is called **Mom Ka Nuska**.
>
> What she makes, she ships. What she cooks, she films — every day. There are no preservatives. There is no factory. Just a real mother, on a real farm, cooking real food, and shipping it to your kitchen.
>
> This is not a food company with a marketing strategy. This is a content engine that happens to sell food."

---

### The Business (60 seconds)

> "Let me give you the numbers.
>
> We're launching with 5 products — 3 pickle varieties and 2 papads. These are categories worth ₹5,000 crore combined. Pantry staples. Every Indian household buys them.
>
> Our gross margin is 55 to 63 percent. That's significantly higher than the industry average of 35-45%. Why? Because we control everything — sourcing from our own farm, production in our own kitchen, no co-packer, no middleman.
>
> Our CAC — customer acquisition cost — is our secret weapon. Because our customers find us through content, not ads.
>
> My mother's daily 'Farm Diary' on Instagram — showing her planting, cooking, packing — that IS the marketing. It costs us zero rupees. When you watch a real person making your food, you don't need a celebrity endorsement.
>
> Year 1 target: ₹40-50 lakhs revenue. Year 3: ₹5-6 crore, EBITDA positive. These are conservative numbers — 10 orders a day in Year 1. That's it."

---

### The Moat (45 seconds)

> "Now, you might ask — what stops someone from copying this?
>
> The answer: authenticity compounds.
>
> On Day 1, our moat is a real mother and a real farm. You can't hire that.
>
> By Month 6, we'll have 5,000 followers watching Mom cook daily. You can't buy that overnight.
>
> By Year 1, we'll have a recipe library of 50+ standardized SOPs covering 28 Indian states. You can't shortcut that.
>
> By Year 3, we'll have a proven playbook for launching food brands — and we'll use it to launch Brand 2, Brand 3, Brand 4.
>
> An investor in Jhaji Store gets a pickle brand. An investor in Mom Ka Nuska gets a brand factory — a repeatable system for launching authentic food brands across India."

---

### The Team (30 seconds)

> "This is a family bet. My mother is the CPO — she owns the product. My sister is Head of Brand — she owns the creative. I'm the CEO — I own strategy, tech, and ops.
>
> We have zero co-founder conflict because we've been a team for life. And here's the thing — in a food brand, the family IS the story. Our customers don't just buy pickles. They buy into a family that cooks together.
>
> And because we're a family team, our burn is lower than any funded D2C startup. Every rupee of your investment goes to growth, not ₹2 lakh monthly CXO salaries."

---

### The Ask — Close Strong (30 seconds)

> "We're raising ₹25 to 50 lakhs.
>
> 40% goes to the farm and kitchen. 20% to the first production run. 20% to marketing. The rest to legal and working capital.
>
> In 18 months, this money gets us: a fully operational farm with content studio, 5 SKUs live on D2C and Amazon, 1,000+ orders delivered, ₹40-50 lakhs in revenue, and — most importantly — ready for a ₹1-2 crore pre-Series A.
>
> We're not looking for just money. We're looking for someone who understands that building a food brand takes patience, and that the payoff — when it comes — is generational.
>
> India needs a food brand it can actually trust. We're building it.
>
> Thank you."

*[Pause. Don't rush. Let it land.]*

## Q&A PREPARATION (Top 10 Questions)

### 1. "You have zero revenue. Why should I invest at ₹2-4 Cr valuation?"

> "You're right — we have zero revenue. But we also have zero waste. Everything pre-capital has been done: team assembled, brand built, market researched, AI ops system built, recipes tested. The valuation reflects a founding team that's all-in, a clear 18-month plan, and a ₹5,000 Cr addressable market. At ₹2-4 Cr, you're getting in before the first product ships. That's the seed premium."

### 2. "Pickles are a commodity. Everyone's grandma makes them."

> "That's exactly the point. Everyone's grandma makes great pickles — but no one has built a BRAND around a real grandma doing it at scale with cameras rolling. The commodity is the product. The brand is the mother, the farm, and the daily proof. We're not competing with ₹50 pickle jars on Amazon. We're competing for the consumer who says 'I want something I can trust.'"

### 3. "Can a mother in a kitchen really scale to ₹5 crore?"

> "Mom doesn't stay on the production line forever. She starts as the sole maker. By Month 6, she's training 2-3 kitchen staff using documented SOPs — every recipe is standardized with exact measurements. By Year 2, her role is R&D, quality control, and content. The recipes are the IP. Mom is the brand face and quality authority. The system does the scaling."

### 4. "D2C food brands struggle with logistics and unit economics."

> "Two things protect us. First — our products are shelf-stable. Pickles last 12 months. Papad lasts 6 months. No cold chain. This dramatically reduces logistics costs. Second — our margins are 55-63%, not the 35-45% most D2C food brands operate at. Because we control sourcing and production. No co-packer taking 15-20% margin."

### 5. "What if something happens to your mother? The brand depends on her."

> "Mom is irreplaceable as a brand figure — but the business isn't dependent on her daily involvement. All recipes are documented as SOPs. The farm and kitchen are operational systems, not one-person shows. Her role transitions to quality authority and content over time. And honestly — this question is why we film EVERYTHING. The content library becomes a permanent asset even if Mom steps back eventually."

### 6. "Family businesses have bad governance. What's your structure?"

> "Pvt Ltd from Day 1. Proper cap table. Founders' agreement with 4-year vesting, 1-year cliff — same as any VC-backed startup. Clear role separation documented. Monthly P&L reporting to investors. Observer board seat for lead investor. Independent CA from Month 1. We may be a family, but we run like a company."

### 7. "What's the exit for me?"

> "Three paths. Most likely: acquisition by an FMCG major in Year 5-8 — this is active in India. ITC bought Sunrise Foods, Tata bought Soulfull, Marico bought True Elements. Second: strategic acquisition by D2C platforms like Amazon or Swiggy building their private label portfolio. Third: if we hit our 15-year vision with 5-8 brands, an IPO. The Indian FMCG M&A market has seen ₹15,000 Cr+ in food brand acquisitions in the last 5 years."

### 8. "Why not just start with marketplaces? Why build a farm?"

> "The farm is not a cost center — it's our biggest asset. It does three things simultaneously: produces ingredients, serves as a production facility, and acts as a content studio. The daily content FROM the farm is what keeps our CAC near zero. Without the farm, we're just another pickle brand on Amazon. With the farm, we're a story people follow."

### 9. "₹25-50 lakhs seems too small. What if you run out?"

> "Our monthly burn is very low — ₹1.5-2L after setup. Family team, no office rent (farm is office), no CXO salaries. At ₹50L raised and ₹15L setup cost, we have 18+ months of runway. And we start generating revenue by Month 7-8. We designed this to be capital efficient — we can reach revenue with minimal burn."

### 10. "Why food? Why not something in tech where you have experience?"

> "Because this is personal. My mother has spent 30 years feeding people with love — and no one outside our family got to experience it. I have the tech skills to scale it. My sister has the creative skills to brand it. We're not building a food company because we read a market report. We're building it because we believe my mother's food deserves to be tasted by the whole country. And it happens to be a ₹5.5 lakh crore market."

## PITCH DELIVERY TIPS

### Body Language
- **Stand, don't sit.** Energy matters.
- **Eye contact** — pick 3 people in the room and cycle between them
- **Hands visible** — gesture naturally, don't cross arms
- **Slow down** at key numbers (margins, TAM, revenue targets)
- **Pause after emotional moments** — let the "mom cooking" image sink in

### Voice
- Start **quiet** with the "close your eyes" hook. Build energy.
- **Emphasize** these words every time: "real mother," "real farm," "zero preservatives," "every day"
- Numbers should be **slow and deliberate**: "Fifty-five to sixty-three percent gross margin."
- End the pitch **lower, not louder.** "We're building it." — then silence.

### Props (If Possible)
- Bring actual product samples in final packaging
- Let investors *taste* the pickle during Q&A
- Have a 30-second video reel of Mom cooking on the farm (phone is fine)

### Don'ts
- Don't apologize for being pre-revenue. Frame it as "pre-capital, not pre-work"
- Don't oversell the tech/AI angle — investors care about the food and the brand, not the internal tools
- Don't compare to Shark Tank companies unless asked
- Don't mention competitors by name in the pitch — only in Q&A if asked
- Don't rush. 5 minutes is enough. Silence is powerful.
